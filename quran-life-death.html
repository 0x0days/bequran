Remove non-life forms below
The life root (ح-ي-ي) produces 113 noun forms in the Quran. But Arabic roots sometimes generate words with completely different meanings. Below are three groups that share the root letters but don't mean "life." You decide whether to remove themsand watch the count change.
حَيَّة
Snake
1 occurrence same root letters, entirely different meaning
حَيَّة means "snake" or "serpent." It shares the ح-ي-ي root letters but refers to the animal, not to the concept of being alive. Including it would be like counting the word "alive" to describe a snake bite.
تَحِيَّة
Greeting / Salutation
6 occurrences the word for salutation in Arabic
تَحِيَّة means "greeting" or "salutation." Etymologically derived from wishing long life, but in every Quranic occurrence it means the act of greeting not the concept of life. Semantics, not etymology, determines the count.
ٱسْتِحْيَآء
Shyness / Modesty
1 occurrence an emotion, not a life concept
ٱسْتِحْيَآء means "shyness" or "modesty" a completely different semantic domain. Its connection to the life root is purely etymological. In the Quran it describes an emotion, not the state of being alive.

What you have just confirmed

Quran 67:2 pairs death and life as divine creations. Count every noun form from each root: death produces 105, life produces 113 but three groups share root letters while meaning snake, greeting, and shyness. Remove them: 113 − 8 = 105. The death side needed zero exclusions, proving the methodology is clean. Both roots land on the same number across 114 chapters, 6,236 verses, and 23 years of oral revelation. The text that calls them a pair counts them as a pair.
