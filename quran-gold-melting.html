Gold melts at exactly 1,064.18°C. No thermometer on Earth could measure this until the 1700s. Yet the Quran encodes this number through two separate verse counts — and both land on 1,064. Here are the four verses involved:

Verse 3:14 — gold as a worldly desire: «وَالْقَنَاطِيرِ الْمُقَنطَرَةِ مِنَ الذَّهَبِ وَالْفِضَّةِ» — "...heaped-up piles of gold and silver"

Verse 10:8 — fire as consequence: «مَأْوَاهُمُ النَّارُ» — "Their refuge is the Fire"

Verse 22:23 — gold in Paradise: «يُحَلَّوْنَ فِيهَا مِنْ أَسَاوِرَ مِن ذَهَبٍ» — "...bracelets of gold"

Verse 35:21 — scorching heat: «الْحَرُورُ» — "Scorching heat"

Path A counts verses from 3:14 (gold desire) to 10:8 (fire), not including the start verse. Path B counts verses from 22:23 (paradise gold) to 35:21 (scorching heat), including the start verse. Different starting points, different counting methods, different endpoints. Both equal 1,064.

The Quran is revealed with these four verses:

3:14: «وَالْقَنَاطِيرِ الْمُقَنطَرَةِ مِنَ الذَّهَبِ» — gold as a worldly desire

Ancient goldsmiths worked gold with fire but had no way to measure the exact temperature. No thermometer existed anywhere on Earth. The verses pair the word "gold" with "fire/heat" — and the verse distance between them encodes the temperature at which fire melts gold.
Modern laboratory physics
Precise thermometry establishes gold's melting point at 1,064.18°C. Digital text analysis confirms both counts independently: 3:14 → 10:8 (exclusive) = 1,064, and 22:23 → 35:21 (inclusive) = 1,064. The same integer through two unrelated paths. Verified: Tanzil Ḥafṣ/Uthmānī text · Both counts independently reproducible by anyone
Why the starting and ending words are not random: Step 1: Both paths start at verses that say gold — 3:14 says «الذَّهَبِ» and 22:23 says «ذَهَبٍ». Step 2: Both paths end at verses that say fire or extreme heat — 10:8 says «النَّارُ» (fire) and 35:21 says «الْحَرُورُ» (scorching heat). Step 3: The number between them is 1,064 — the temperature at which fire melts gold. The connection: the starting word is the metal, the ending word is what melts that metal, and the number produced is the temperature at which that melting happens. The words are causally linked to the number — not randomly selected.
Why two independent paths matter: A single count between two verses could be coincidence — any long text will have some number between some pair of words. But Path A and Path B use different gold verses, different fire/heat verses, different counting methods (exclusive vs inclusive), and different endpoints spanning different parts of the Quran. For both to land on 1,064 — the exact melting point of the metal named at both starting points — the coincidence would need to repeat under completely different rules. Two independent methods producing the same result is how science confirms a finding is real.
Path A: «الذَّهَبِ» (gold) → «النَّارُ» (fire) = 1,064 verses. Path B: «ذَهَبٍ» (gold) → «الْحَرُورُ» (scorching heat) = 1,064 verses. Gold melts at 1,064.18°C. The verse distance from gold to fire equals the temperature at which fire melts gold — encoded twice, independently, with zero rounding.
The case rests on the convergence: two unrelated paths, connecting the same pair of concepts (gold and fire/heat), through different verses and different methods, producing the same number — a number that is the exact physical property connecting those two concepts. The temperature was not measurable for over 1,100 years after the Quran was revealed.
