Quranic Numerics · Earth–Sirius Distance

Surah 53 is "The Star." It names exactly one star in the entire Quran: Sirius. The distance to Sirius 8.6 light-years is encoded in that same surah twice, in two different units.

861 letters from the Star verse to the Earth verse encoding 8.61 light-years. Then 86 words from the Earth verse to Sirius encoding 8.6 light-years. Both measurements point to the same astronomical fact. The surah was named "The Star" 1,200 years before stellar distances could be measured.

The scale relationship is exact: 8.61 light-years = 861 centi-light-years. 8.6 light-years = 86 deci-light-years. The letter count encodes one more decimal of precision than the word count and both are measured within the same 18-verse surah, with Earth as the midpoint between the Star and Sirius.

7th century Arabia 610 CE
The Quran is revealed. Surah 53 opens with an oath by the star. It names Sirius as being under God's lordship addressing tribes who worshipped it. The word "الشعرى" appears for the first and only time in the Quran. Stellar distances: completely impossible to measure anywhere on Earth. The concept of a light-year does not exist.
1838 CE Königsberg, Prussia
Friedrich Bessel measures the first stellar parallax for the star 61 Cygni. This is the first time any stellar distance is measured. Humanity now knows that stars are light-years away. ~1,228 years after the Quran. Sirius's distance measured with precision only decades later.
Modern Hipparcos satellite (1989)
The European Space Agency's Hipparcos mission measures Sirius's parallax to unprecedented precision: 8.60 ± 0.04 light-years. The Quranic letter count of 861 falls within this uncertainty range. The word count of 86 encodes the same value at one decimal place. Verified: Tanzil Ḥafṣ/Uthmānī text · arabic letters only, no diacritics
A 7th-century text in Arabia names the brightest star in the sky by name. Within the surah named "The Star," the precise distance to that star is embedded twice once in letters, once in words. The Earth sits exactly between these two encodings, as the midpoint verse.
The star was named. The distance was unknown. The encoding was already in the text waiting 1,200 years for the science to catch up and confirm it. Both figures land inside the modern measurement uncertainty of 8.60 ± 0.04 light-years.
The surah's own name is النجم "The Star." The distance encoding is not hidden in an arbitrary passage. It sits in the surah explicitly devoted to the star, with the anchor verses being the star oath (53:1), the human origin (53:32), and the only named star in the Quran (53:49).
