The Quran names a mechanism for crossing cosmic distances in steps. Einstein discovered the same mechanism 1,300 years later.

A wormhole is a shortcut through spacetime — it folds the universe so that two points separated by millions of light-years become reachable in a few steps. Einstein described this in 1935, calling them "bridges" in space-time. The Quran calls them Ma'arej (معارج — ascending pathways) and describes how angels use them for long-distance travel — including a precise time-dilation ratio matching what general relativity predicts.

What "Ma'arej" means: The Arabic word معارج literally means "ascending steps" or "stairways going up." It describes something you walk through step by step to cross a great distance — a remarkably precise description of what a wormhole does: it creates a traversable path through which enormous distances become passable in steps.
The bizarre scenery in Quran 15:15: The Quran adds that a traveler passing through this door would see towering heavenly structures and not believe they had truly reached them — they would think they were optical illusions. General relativity predicts exactly this: relativistic effects inside a wormhole make it impossible to judge how far you have actually traveled.
"Owner of the Ma'arej." Three words naming a mechanism physicists discovered in the 20th century. The time ratio, the length contraction, the opening doors — all described in the 7th century.
Einstein formulated general relativity in 1915 and described wormholes in 1935. The Quran used the word Ma'arej 1,300 years earlier — alongside a precise time-dilation ratio and a description of length contraction that no 7th-century human had any basis to formulate.
