Kármán Line · where atmosphere ends

Wind is the movement of atmospheric gas. It requires air molecules to exist. Earth's atmosphere — the only gas envelope we have — extends roughly 100 km upward. Above that line is outer space: a near-vacuum with no gas, no clouds, and no wind. The Quran's description "between the sky and earth" places wind exclusively within this atmospheric zone, a fact no 7th century human could verify by going there.

Modern Atmospheric Science
Wind is the movement of atmospheric gas molecules. Earth's atmosphere — from the surface to roughly 100 km altitude (the Kármán Line) — is the only region dense enough to support wind as a phenomenon.

Beyond the Kármán Line: outer space is an extreme vacuum. Gas molecule density drops to near zero. Atmospheric wind ceases to exist. The ISS orbits at ~400 km in total silence.

What about solar wind? "Solar wind" is a stream of charged plasma particles ejected by the Sun — not the movement of atmospheric gas. It is a different physical phenomenon entirely. Atmospheric wind (what the Quran and modern meteorology both refer to) requires gas molecules and exists only within a planetary atmosphere.
Why is the Kármán Line 100 km? At 100 km altitude, the atmosphere is too thin for a conventional aircraft to generate lift — the medium that carries wind no longer behaves as a fluid. This boundary was identified by engineer Theodore von Kármán and adopted by the Fédération Aéronautique Internationale. It corresponds to where atmospheric phenomena — including wind and clouds — cease to exist.
The Quran says winds are controlled "between the sky and the earth." Modern atmospheric science confirms that wind — the movement of gas — exists exclusively within Earth's atmosphere, ending at the Kármán Line 100 km up.
In the 7th century, no human had ever left the atmosphere to observe this. The only way to know wind does not exist beyond it is to go there — or to receive that knowledge from another source. The statement was verified for the first time when humans reached space in the 20th century.
