Lesson · Pulsars & Black Holes

Quranic Numerics · Astrophysics

Two Arabic words in one verse describe a knocking star that makes a hole. Science calls them pulsars and black holes.

الثَّاقِبُ

Pulsars are rotating neutron stars that emit regular radio pulses. Connect a radio telescope to a speaker and you hear a persistent knocking sound. The Arabic word Tarek means "the one who knocks." The same verse then calls this star Thakeb from the root thukb (ثقب), meaning "hole." When a neutron star gains enough mass, it collapses and tears a hole in spacetime a black hole. Two words. Two precise properties. One verse written 1,400 years before either was discovered.

الطَّارِقُ
Knock · Knock · Knock
Pulsars emit radio waves at precise, regular intervals. When recorded and played through a speaker, they sound exactly like someone persistently knocking. Astronomers first detected them in 1967 but the Quran called this star "the knocker" in the 7th century.

The root word thukb (ثقب) means "hole." Thakeb means "the one that makes a hole." As a neutron star gains mass, gravity eventually overwhelms all other forces the star collapses and tears a hole in spacetime. This is a black hole. The Quran links the knocker to the hole-maker in the same breath.
One verse. Two properties. One type of star.

Same star, one verse 86:3 Neutron star → Black hole
The knocking sound is real: Pulsars are the most precise natural clocks in the universe. They spin up to 700 times per second, emitting radio pulses with millisecond accuracy. When converted to audio, slow pulsars sound like steady, rhythmic knocking. The Arabic word tarek is the same root used for "someone knocking at the door."
One star, two fates: A neutron star with enough mass inevitably collapses into a black hole. The Quran linked both phenomena the knocking and the hole-making to the same star in a single line. Astrophysicists confirmed this exact relationship in the 20th century.
"The Knocker the piercing star." Three words. Two scientific discoveries. One verse from the 7th century.
Pulsars were first detected in 1967 by Jocelyn Bell Burnell. Black holes were theorized in 1915 and confirmed in the 20th century. No human in 7th-century Arabia had any basis for describing a star that knocks rhythmically and also makes a hole in space yet the Quran described both in the same breath.
