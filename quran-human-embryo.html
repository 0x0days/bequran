Quranic Numerics · Human Embryology

Two words in a 7th-century text describe the human embryo with an accuracy that required a microscope to confirm.

Quran 23:14 Surah Al-Mu'minun · The Believers
ثُمَّ خَلَقْنَا النُّطْفَةَ عَلَقَةً فَخَلَقْنَا الْعَلَقَةَ مُضْغَةً فَخَلَقْنَا الْمُضْغَةَ عِظَامًا فَكَسَوْنَا الْعِظَامَ لَحْمًا ثُمَّ أَنْشَأْنَاهُ خَلْقًا آخَرَ
"Then We developed the semen into a leech. Then We developed the leech into a chewed lump. Then We developed the lump into bones. Then We clothed the bones with flesh. Then We produced it into another creature."
عَلَقَةً
Alaqa
Leech
The early embryo (days 23–24) clings to the uterine wall to feed exactly as a leech clings to a host. Under a microscope, its shape is also curved and leech-like.
مُضْغَةً
Mudgha
Something Chewed
At week 6, the developing vertebrae (somites) create a surface that looks exactly like teeth marks pressed into soft clay or flesh.
🔬
No microscope existed in 7th-century Arabia. The embryo at these stages is smaller than a grain of rice. These developmental details were invisible to the naked eye and unknown to any civilization of that era verified for the first time when microscopy became available in the 20th century.

Alaqa shape and behavior both match
Embryo days 23–24
A leech
The word Alaqa captures a double accuracy:
Shape: the embryo curves into the same elongated form as a leech.
Behavior: it buries into the uterine wall and draws nutrients from the mother's blood precisely as a leech attaches to a host and feeds.

Both resemblances were only visible after microscopy was developed.
Mudgha somites that look like bite marks
Embryo vs. chewed gum
Chewed gum
At week 6, the embryo develops somites paired blocks of tissue that form along the spine. These create a bumpy, indented surface that looks exactly like teeth marks pressed into a piece of clay or soft flesh. The Arabic word Mudgha means precisely that: something that has been chewed. The visual match was confirmed by modern microscopy.

Gap between Quran and confirmation ~1,400 years
Why "leech" is a double match: Most ancient descriptions of embryology (Greek, Indian, Egyptian) used metaphors from seeds, animals, or fluids none used "leech." The leech metaphor captures both the visual shape and the parasitic feeding mechanism simultaneously. Choosing this one word required knowing both facts neither visible without magnification.
A text from the 7th century described two microscopic stages of human development using words so precise that modern embryologists recognize both immediately leech for the implantation stage, and chewed for the somite stage.
No microscope. No laboratory. No scientific tradition of embryology anywhere in 7th-century Arabia. The Quran's two-word description outperforms the entire ancient world's understanding of embryonic development by over a millennium.
