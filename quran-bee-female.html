Pattern 2b · The Female Worker

The Quran commands the worker bees in the feminine. In 7th-century Arabia, hive workers were assumed to be male.

Arabic grammar marks gender on every verb and command. When the Quran speaks directly to the bee in Surah 16:68–69, it uses three imperatives. All three are in the feminine form, which happens to be exactly what biology later confirmed about who does the work inside a hive.

Quran 16:68–69 · feminine imperatives highlighted
وَأَوْحَىٰ رَبُّكَ إِلَى ٱلنَّحْلِ أَنِ ٱتَّخِذِى مِنَ ٱلْجِبَالِ بُيُوتًا وَمِنَ ٱلشَّجَرِ وَمِمَّا يَعْرِشُونَ ﴿٦٨﴾ ثُمَّ كُلِى مِن كُلِّ ٱلثَّمَرَٰتِ فَٱسْلُكِى سُبُلَ رَبِّكِ ذُلُلًا ﴿٦٩﴾

7th-century assumption
Hive workers were believed to be male like soldiers in a human army. A human author would have used masculine commands.
What the Quran says
Every command addressed to the bee uses the feminine form. The text treats the worker bee as female three times, consistently.
When was this confirmed? The sex of worker bees was first established by Dutch naturalist Jan Swammerdam around 1673, and conclusively confirmed in the 18th century. It was not common knowledge in 7th-century Arabia. Arab culture associated organised work-groups with males.
William Shakespeare himsefl assumed wronly that the working bees were male, and hives are ruled by a male bee kings. Referenced in his play Henry V (Act 1, Scene 2)
The noun نَحْل (bee) is grammatically masculine in Arabic. A 7th-century human author following convention would write masculine commands. The Quran writes feminine ones and is biologically correct.
Not once, not ambiguously. Three consecutive commands, all using the feminine imperative suffix -ī, all addressed to the bee that actually builds, forages, and produces honey. The text carries a biological accuracy that could not have been assumed from the culture it was revealed into.
